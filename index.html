Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)

PCOS is a hormonal condition where the ovaries produce excess androgens (male hormones). It affects 1 in 10 women of reproductive age and is one of the most common causes of irregular periods.

What causes PCOS?

  • Insulin resistance — the body doesn't use insulin efficiently
  • Excess androgen production by the ovaries
  • Genetic factors — it often runs in families
  • Low-grade inflammation

Can PCOS be cured?

PCOS has no cure, but symptoms can be effectively managed through medication, diet, exercise, and lifestyle changes. Many women with PCOS live full, healthy lives.

Common symptoms

  • Irregular or missed periods
  • Weight gain, especially around the belly
  • Acne or oily skin
  • Excess facial or body hair (hirsutism)
  • Thinning hair or hair loss on the scalp
  • Darkened skin patches (acanthosis nigricans)
  • Difficulty getting pregnant
  • Mood swings, anxiety, or depression

Foods to eat more of 💚

  • High-fiber: broccoli, leafy greens, lentils
  • Lean protein: chicken, fish, tofu, eggs
  • Anti-inflammatory: berries, tomatoes, olive oil
  • Low-GI carbs: brown rice, sweet potato, oats
  • Omega-3: salmon, flaxseed, walnuts

Foods to limit 🚫

  • Refined carbs: white bread, pasta, pastries
  • Sugary drinks and fruit juices
  • Processed and fast food
  • High-fat red meats
  • Excess dairy (may raise androgen levels)

Helpful supplements

  • Inositol — improves insulin sensitivity
  • Magnesium — reduces insulin resistance
  • Vitamin D — often low in PCOS
  • Omega-3 — reduces inflammation
  • Zinc — helps with hair and acne

Exercise

  • Aim for 30 min of moderate exercise, 5x a week
  • Strength training improves insulin sensitivity
  • Low-impact cardio: walking, swimming, cycling
  • Yoga and Pilates reduce cortisol
  • Avoid over-exercising — it raises cortisol

Sleep & stress

  • Aim for 7–9 hours of sleep nightly
  • Poor sleep worsens insulin resistance
  • Practice stress reduction: meditation, journaling
  • High cortisol worsens hormone imbalances

When to see a doctor 🩺

  • Missed periods for 3+ months
  • Sudden or severe weight gain
  • Trying to get pregnant
  • Worsening symptoms despite lifestyle changes

Common PCOS medications

  • Metformin — improves insulin sensitivity; take with food
  • Birth control pills — regulates periods, reduces androgens
  • Spironolactone — reduces hair growth and acne
  • Clomiphene / Letrozole — for ovulation induction

Important reminders 💊

  • Take pills at the same time daily
  • Never stop medication without consulting your doctor
  • Some medications take weeks to show effect
  • Report side effects to your healthcare provider
  • Regular blood tests may be needed for Metformin
